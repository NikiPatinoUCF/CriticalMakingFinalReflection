Start Here

a final reflection  ·  ENG 6819 Critical Making  ·  Spring 2026

There is a canvas in my house that has been painted over a dozen times. On its back: To: Darcy. From: Julianne. Darcy was my best friend. She passed away shortly after meeting my daughter, and her mother returned the canvas to us. We keep painting over it. Every new image holds every previous one in its layers. The inscription stays.

I started this semester on that canvas, and I didn't know I was also describing what was ahead: a surface that accumulates, that makes the new image possible only because of what's underneath.

✦

I came in with a novel in progress, a theoretical frame, and no method. I expected critical making to mean learning tools. I learned to draw stars in p5.js by changing numbers until the sky looked right. I folded a zine, rejected it, and built a comic structure that let two stories run parallel without collapsing into one. I traced figures onto construction paper because I needed to commit. No undo button, just a line that counted or didn't. I built a Twine index of 41 cards for a story I'll never write, with dead ends and one REDACTED entry the reader searches for and never finds.

What I did not expect was that the tools would keep making the same argument.

Every project, without my planning it, circled the same question: what happens when a system encounters something it cannot process?

The DMV Bitsy had no AI license. The resume activity showed an AI quietly amputating fifteen years of my life to produce a coherent story. The GIF implicated my own hands in the cycle of cognitive offloading. The corpus analysis found that discipline appears in my writing alone across sixty-six submissions about transformation. I didn't plan the throughline. The making revealed it.

✦

The Voyant week changed something specific. I ran my own writing against sixty-six submissions, and the ternary plot showed my pieces sitting alone at the center, where the institutional, the systemic, and the corporeal all converge. No other writer in the corpus holds that position. Computational distance had done something close reading never could: it showed me my own writing from the outside, as a consistent signature, a location in a field.

The center of that visualization is nearly empty. That space is Machine Gospel. Now I can see it. Now I can write it.

✦

The semester taught me that material resists. Spices oxidize. Glitter persists. Paper tears. The code produces the star only when you change the right number, found by changing the wrong ones first. I kept choosing materials that pushed back, and the resistance was always doing something. The comic's human meter approaches perfection and never arrives. That gap is not illustrated by the layout. It is the layout. Loveless writes that work should embody the conditions it responds to, not just represent them. When I met that standard, I knew. When I didn't, the material let me know first.

I also learned that invisibility is a design decision. REDACTED is not a failure. It is a choice. Some things are not meant to be processed, and that is also the line I am learning to hold: what to name, what to leave unnamed, and how to make the withholding mean something.

✦

The final portfolio, Rites of the Irreducible, taught me that the word irreducible didn't mean what I needed it to mean until I made the thing I was trying to name. I landed on computable: the irreducible human interior is not what resists abstract simplification but what resists specific computation. The word became mine only through the making.

Philip said: It's not a revolution if the power structures don't change. I am still sitting with that. My Google map didn't change the power structures I was up against, but it made the road visible. My corpus analysis located the gap I could fill. My Twine index performed the searching. Making is not revolution itself, but it might be the infrastructure that makes revolution imaginable.

What this semester gave me is not a plan but a sensibility: the willingness to make before I understand, to let the resistance of the material tell me what I'm arguing. I know that the making is the research, not the illustration of it. I know that the canvas accumulates. That what's painted over stays.

I don't know yet what I'm making next.
I know I'm making something.

Nicole Patino  ·  ENG 6819 Critical Making, Spring 2026  ·  University of Central Florida
